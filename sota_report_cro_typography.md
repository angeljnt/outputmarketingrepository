# SOTA Report: CRO Typography and Text Formatting for Conversion
Date: 2026-03-24
Scope: What text formatting, sizing, emphasis, alignment, and layout decisions maximize engagement and conversion on marketing websites — specifically for boutique agency and B2B SaaS contexts.

---

## 1. Executive Summary

- Users read at most 20-28% of text on any page. Every formatting decision must serve skimmers first, readers second.
- F-pattern scanning means the first 2 words of every line carry disproportionate weight. Front-load everything.
- Bold text and numbers are "spotted" during scanning — they act as fixation magnets before any body copy is read.
- Body text below 16px creates a measurable trust penalty and is an accessibility failure. 17-20px is the CRO-optimized baseline.
- Never center text longer than 2 lines. Centering long body copy increases cognitive load and kills comprehension.
- Dark/contrast standalone sections have conversion power — but only if used 1-2 times per page maximum.
- Social proof placed immediately before or after a CTA produces a documented 35.6% increase in conversion.

---

## 2. How Users Actually Read (The Foundation)

### The scanning patterns — which one applies when

| Pattern | When it appears | What to do |
|---------|-----------------|-----------|
| F-pattern | Text-heavy pages | Left column + first 2 words of every line are most-read; front-load key claims |
| Z-pattern | Landing pages / sparse text | Top-left → top-right → diagonal → bottom-right; CTAs at Z endpoint |
| Spotted | During skimming (all pages) | Bold text + numbers are fixated even mid-skim; use them for key data |
| Commitment | Only when content feels highly relevant | Only earned — not designed for |
| Layer cake | Medium-density pages | Headings scanned, body skipped; subheadings must tell the full story alone |

**Implication for writing**: Every subheading must be self-sufficient. A reader scanning only headings should understand the entire page.

### What gets seen vs what gets skipped

| Position | Gets seen? | What to put there |
|----------|------------|-------------------|
| First 2 words of any line | Always | Key term, benefit, or differentiator |
| Line endings | Often skipped | Supporting detail only |
| Subheadings | Almost always scanned | Self-sufficient — the claim, not a label |
| First sentence of paragraph | High probability | Lead with the claim, never bury it |
| Numbers / bold text anywhere | Very high — spotted pattern | Stats, metrics, key phrases |
| Below 50% scroll depth | Seen by ~50% of visitors | Never put the only CTA here |
| Below 30% scroll depth | Seen by <50% | Critical claims must appear in the first or second screenful |

## 3. Font Sizes — The Full Decision Table

### Desktop and mobile sizing (CRO-optimized, not just accessible)

| Element | Desktop | Mobile | Notes |
|---------|---------|--------|-------|
| Hero headline (H1) | 56–72px | 36–44px | One per page; must read in one pass above the fold |
| Section header (H2) | 36–44px | 24–32px | Visible during a scroll scan |
| Card / item header (H3) | 20–24px | 18–22px | Within a section |
| Body (intro / emphasis contexts) | 18–20px | 16–18px | Above WCAG minimum — signals professionalism |
| Body (standard) | 17–20px | 16–18px | 16px is the floor; going to 18-20px produces trust lift |
| Overline / label | 11–13px | 11–12px | Context-setter only |
| Footer / caption | 13–14px | 12–13px | Supporting info only |

**Why going above 16px matters**: Several CRO practitioners report measurable conversion lift from body text increases alone. 18-20px signals professionalism and trust. One documented case: changing from 10px to 13px improved conversions measurably. Going from 16px to 18px is a lower-risk, higher-return move.

**Rule**: Each typographic level must be visually obvious. A reader should never confuse H2 with H3 at a glance. If you have to look twice, the hierarchy has failed.

## 4. What to Bold — The Exact Rules

### Bold for:
- Specific outcomes with numbers: "7 brands added to waitlist" — bold the number AND the outcome
- Risk reducers that lower friction: "No retainer. No contract." — bold these exactly
- Key differentiators in a competitive claim
- The single most important phrase in a section (one per paragraph maximum)
- CTA text (by default — all CTAs carry bold weight)
- Metrics inside testimonials: "140+ paying customers in 6 months" — bold the number

### Do NOT bold:
- Entire sentences or paragraphs — destroys the signal
- Adjectives without specifics ("proven," "best," "exceptional")
- Features instead of outcomes
- Random words for visual texture
- More than one phrase per paragraph (scarcity principle — bold loses power when overused)

**The rule**: Bold = scarcity principle applied to text. If everything is highlighted, nothing stands out. Bold is a claim that something is important. Make it rare enough to be believed.

## 5. Text Alignment — When to Center, When to Left

| Element | Rule | Reason |
|---------|------|--------|
| Body copy / paragraphs | LEFT always | Centered long text forces the brain to search for line starts — cognitive load goes up, comprehension goes down |
| Hero headline (1-2 lines) | CENTER or LEFT (test) | Acceptable when short enough to read in one visual pass |
| Section headers (H2) | CENTER acceptable | Short, signals topic shift, readable |
| Subheadings inside body | LEFT | Part of reading flow |
| Standalone CTAs | CENTER | Draws eye to action; full-width on mobile |
| Overlines / labels | CENTER acceptable | 1-5 words; sets context |
| Long testimonials | LEFT | Must be readable — centered multi-line text fails |
| Pull quotes (1-2 sentences) | CENTER acceptable | Short + isolated; centering adds emphasis |
| Statistics / metric blocks | CENTER | Single number or data point; creates visual anchor |

**The rule of thumb**: Never center text longer than 2 lines in body copy. Center only what can be consumed in a single visual fixation.

**Joanna Wiebe's addition**: When controlling line breaks on a centered headline, ensure the first 2 words of line 1 AND line 2 are the most important words in the sentence. Never let a filler word anchor a line.

## 6. Pull Quotes — What They Do and When to Use Them

**Function**: Extract a short passage from body copy, isolate it visually at a larger size. Gives skimmers the gist; slows scrollers; rewards readers with emphasis.

**When to use**:
- When there is one sentence that summarizes the core reason to buy — pull it out
- When a testimonial contains a specific metric — isolate it
- When a section is long and a skimmer would miss the point — surface it

**What makes a good pull quote**:
- 1-3 sentences maximum
- Contains a specific claim or number — not a vague statement
- Is the most important sentence in the section, not a decorative one
- Larger font (typically 20-28px), often a different weight or color

**What not to pull**:
- Generic statements ("We love working with them")
- Anything that requires context from the surrounding paragraph to make sense

## 7. Dark / Standalone Sections — The Interrupt Strategy

**What they do**: A dark or high-contrast section creates a visual interrupt. The reader's eye "wakes up" after the contrast shift. It resets attention. Used correctly, it directs focus to the one thing the visitor must not miss.

**Use for** (ranked by CRO value):
1. The single most important conversion statement on the page — the "why act now" moment
2. Risk reducers immediately before or after a CTA ("No retainer. No contract.")
3. A social proof stat or testimonial that earns trust before the CTA
4. A section transition between problem and solution

**Rules**:
- Maximum 2 dark sections per page. One is often better.
- Each dark section carries ONE message. Not a list. Not multiple ideas.
- 70-80% of the page should be light. Dark sections derive their power from contrast with the rest.
- More than 2 dark sections = desensitization; each loses impact.

## 8. Overlines — The Underused CRO Tool

**What they are**: Small labels (10-13px, uppercase, wider letter-spacing, muted color) placed above a headline.

**Function**: Context-setter. Tells the reader what category or topic they're entering before they read the headline. Primes their mental model so the headline lands harder.

**Examples**:
- "The Work" above a case studies header
- "Why it works" above a methodology section
- "No retainer. No contract." above a pricing section headline

**CRO value**: Priming increases message retention. A reader who knows what category they're reading processes the headline with less friction and more credulity.

## 9. WCAG Minimums (The Floor, Not the Target)

| Standard | Requirement | CRO implication |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| WCAG 2.1 SC 1.4.12 (AA) | Line height ≥ 1.5x font size | CRO-optimized sites hit 1.5–2.0x; 1.5 is the floor |
| WCAG 2.1 SC 1.4.12 (AA) | Paragraph spacing ≥ 2x font size | Creates visual breathing room between ideas |
| WCAG 2.1 SC 1.4.3 (AA) | Contrast 4.5:1 for normal text; 3:1 for large text | Any emphasis color must clear this before being used |
| WCAG 2.1 SC 1.4.6 (AAA) | 7:1 contrast (normal); 4.5:1 (large) | Premium standard; use when possible for body text |
| Line length | Under 80 characters (WCAG-aligned) | NN/G optimal: 50–75 characters |
| Body text minimum | 16px (AA-aligned practice) | 18-20px recommended for conversion pages |

## 10. Anti-Patterns — What Kills Conversion Through Poor Text Formatting

| Anti-pattern | Conversion damage |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Walls of body text with no visual breaks | Skimmers bounce before reaching the CTA |
| Centering long body paragraphs | Increased cognitive load; comprehension drops |
| Bolding random phrases | Destroys bold's ability to signal importance |
| More than 2 dark/callout sections per page | Desensitization; each section loses impact |
| Hero headline below 36px | Fails the "read in one pass" test above the fold |
| Body text below 16px | Trust penalty; accessibility failure |
| Sections with no overlines | Reader has no context before they hit the headline |
| Primary CTA placed only below 50% scroll | Seen by fewer than half of all visitors |
| Multiple CTAs at equal visual weight | Decision paralysis; visitor doesn't know what to do |
| No whitespace between sections | Sections bleed together; hierarchy collapses |
| Testimonials without specific metrics | Vague social proof adds less than specific social proof |
| Pull quotes with no specific claim | Visual texture with no conversion function |

## 11. Social Proof — Placement and Formatting Rules

**Proximity to CTA**: Social proof placed immediately before OR after a CTA produces a documented 35.6% conversion increase. Do not put testimonials in a separate section far from the CTA.

**Format hierarchy**:
1. Featured testimonial (large, pulled out, specific metric) — highest conversion value
2. Pull quote with metric ("7 brands added to waitlist") — strong
3. Inline testimonial (standard body size) — baseline
4. Generic quote without metric — lowest value; reconsider including it

**Specificity rule**: "7 brands added to waitlist" outperforms "great results." Specificity = credibility. Always push clients for exact numbers.

**Numbers inside testimonials**: Bold the metric. "We went from **67 signups to 2,100 per month**" is more credible than the same sentence without bold — the number is spotted immediately and anchors the reader's attention.

## 12. The Readability Benchmark

**Flesch Reading Ease score** for marketing sites: 60–90 (6th–8th grade reading level).

This is not dumbing down. This is removing friction. A marketing page is not an academic paper. Every extra syllable is friction.

**Portent empirical study (2021)**: Readability explains approximately 11% of conversion rate variance across sites. It is not the dominant factor — but it is a consistent one.

**Practical translation**:
- Short sentences over long ones
- Anglo-Saxon words over Latinate ones ("use" over "utilize," "help" over "facilitate")
- Active voice over passive
- One idea per sentence

## 13. The Page-Level Framework — How Everything Connects

### What the first screenful must do
- Hero H1 readable in one pass (56-72px desktop)
- The value proposition established before any scroll
- A CTA visible above the fold or within the first scroll
- Social proof signal (logo bar, credential) visible in the first screenful

### What the body of the page must do
- Subheadings must tell the story on their own (layer cake scanning)
- First sentence of every paragraph leads with the claim
- Bold text and metrics appear at the points of highest importance
- One dark/interrupt section maximum in the body — use for the single most important CRO moment
- Social proof placed immediately before the primary CTA

### What the CTA sections must do
- Single most-wanted action is visually dominant — one CTA, not three
- Risk reducers adjacent to the CTA ("No retainer. No contract.")
- If two CTAs coexist, visual weight must clearly differentiate primary from secondary
